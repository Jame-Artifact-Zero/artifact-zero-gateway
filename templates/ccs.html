§0

Preamble

RGB defined how screens display light. CMYK defined how ink meets paper. Neither defined how words carry structural intent.

Every sentence a human writes contains emotional architecture — patterns of comfort, pressure, avoidance, dominance, collapse, overreach, drift, and manufactured trust. These patterns are invisible to the reader. They are not invisible to geometry.

In 1980, Robert Plutchik published a psychoevolutionary theory identifying eight primary emotions arranged as four opposing pairs. He assigned each a color. He proved that emotions blend like pigments — joy and trust produce love, anger and anticipation produce aggression.

AZ-CCS takes Plutchik's wheel and completes it: each emotion maps to a structural communication failure mode detected by the NTI (No-Tilt Interface) engine. The color isn't decorative. The color is the diagnosis.

When a child sees yellow highlighting on text, they know: someone is being comforted instead of informed. When they see red, someone is asserting authority without evidence. When they see indigo, the message has collapsed into vagueness. The color teaches before the word explains.

Color Specification

Each emotion color exists at three intensities. The intensity determines where the color is used in interface rendering.

Intensity Tiers

TIER 1 — GLOW
Background / Highlight
12% opacity of the signal color on void background. Used for: text highlight backgrounds, card backgrounds, hover states, inline annotation backgrounds. Must never fight the text for attention.
TIER 2 — SIGNAL
Border / Badge / Icon
Full saturation. The color you see. Used for: left-border accents, badge text, category tags, chart elements, the wheel petals themselves. This is the identifying color.
TIER 3 — DEEP
Text on Light / Dark Anchoring
Darkened variant for text legibility on light backgrounds, or for deep shadows and emphasis. Used for: text inside glow-background cards, print mode, accessibility contrast.

§3

The Nine Laws

These are the immutable rules of AZ-CCS. Any implementation claiming compliance must satisfy all nine.

LAW 1
One Emotion, One Hue Family
Each Plutchik emotion maps to exactly one hue. Joy is always yellow. Anger is always red. No exceptions. No "themes" that reassign colors. The mapping is permanent.
LAW 2
Three Tiers, Always Three
Every emotion color exists as Glow (background), Signal (UI element), and Deep (text). No additional tiers. No "medium" or "light-medium." Three is the number.
LAW 3
Glow Never Exceeds 15% Opacity
Background highlights use the Signal color at 10-12% opacity on the void background. This ensures text remains primary. The glow suggests — it never shouts.
LAW 4
Structural Green Is Reserved
#00e89c belongs to one concept: structural integrity confirmed. It is NOT an emotion color. It is the absence of tilt. Clean. It appears only when no failure mode is detected.
LAW 5
No Amber Without Meaning
The legacy "amber = warning" usage is retired. Orange (#f97316) now means Anticipation/Pressure — artificial urgency. If you see orange, someone is forcing a timeline. This is not a generic caution signal.
LAW 6
Opposites Must Be Visually Opposite
Joy/Sadness (yellow/indigo), Trust/Disgust (lime/magenta), Fear/Anger (green/red), Surprise/Anticipation (cyan/orange). In any visualization, opposing emotions must sit across from each other. The spatial relationship carries meaning.
LAW 7
Intensity Follows Plutchik
Darker = more intense, exactly as Plutchik specified. Annoyance is lighter red than Rage. Pensiveness is lighter indigo than Grief. Saturation and darkness increase together toward the emotional core.
LAW 8
Dyads Inherit Both Parents
When two adjacent emotions co-occur, the dyad is rendered using both parent colors — split border, gradient, or side-by-side swatches. Dyads never get their own unique color. The parentage is always visible.
LAW 9
The Standard Is the Standard
AZ-CCS hex values are fixed. They do not adapt to brand guidelines, user preferences, or dark/light mode. The emotion colors remain constant. The chrome (void, surface, border) may adapt. The Eight do not change.
§4

Primary Dyads

When two adjacent emotions appear in the same text, they form a dyad — a compound structural pattern. Plutchik identified these as complex emotions. In communication, they represent compound failure modes.

Optimism
Joy + Trust
"Don't worry, everyone agrees." Reassurance fused with manufactured consensus. Double insulation from reality.
Submission
Trust + Fear
"Best practice says we should defer this." Authority cited to justify avoidance. The constraint dies with credibility attached.
Awe
Fear + Surprise
Deferral meets scope drift. Constraint pushed to "later" while the conversation silently moves to new territory. The original is lost twice.
Disapproval
Surprise + Sadness
Scope expanded into vagueness. New topic introduced AND immediately blended into "overall" summary. Maximum entropy.
Remorse
Sadness + Disgust
Collapsed categories combined with overreach claims. "Basically, it handles everything overall." Vagueness and violation fused.
Contempt
Disgust + Anger
Overreach enforced by authority. "Our solution handles everything — experts agree." Boundary violation plus silencing.
Aggression
Anger + Anticipation
"Research shows we need this ASAP." Authority imposed with artificial urgency. The most forceful compound pattern.
Optimism
Anticipation + Joy
"We need to move fast — don't worry, it'll be fine." Pressure softened by reassurance. Speed disguised as comfort.
